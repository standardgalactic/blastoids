## The Compiled Self

The concept of the **compiled self** is introduced as a formal structure derived from irreversible historical processes. Rather than treating identity as a static object, it is defined as a dynamically maintained abstraction whose stability depends on strict structural conditions.

The framework is developed through a sequence of four theorems, each establishing a necessary component for the existence of a stable identity.

---

## Core Theorems

### 1. The Abstraction Theorem

A stable interface exists if and only if a constraint functional enforces global coherence across the system’s history.

This theorem establishes abstraction as a structural necessity. A system can only present a stable representation if its evolution is constrained such that its history remains internally consistent.

---

### 2. The Substrate Theorem

A symbolic medium functions as a coherent substrate if and only if it preserves spectrally detectable generative distinctions through a paired encoding–decoding structure.

This requires that the medium retain sufficient structure to reconstruct historical trajectories and distinguish between distinct generative processes after encoding.

---

### 3. The Policy Theorem

A policy is viable over unbounded time if and only if it simultaneously satisfies constraint preservation and the non-collapse of admissible future volume.

This theorem formalizes system dynamics. Viability requires maintaining coherence while ensuring that at least one valid future trajectory always remains available. This introduces a necessary balance between restrictive and expansive operations.

---

### 4. The Main Theorem

A compiled self exists if and only if the conditions of the Abstraction, Substrate, and Policy theorems are jointly satisfied, and a narrative type system enforces admissibility at each transition.

This theorem integrates the prior results into a single biconditional definition, establishing the compiled self as a structurally constrained and locally validated process.

---

## Interpretation

Under this framework, the self is not a substance but a **provenance-stable interface**. It is an ongoing achievement of constrained dynamics that preserves identity, temporality, and external legibility through the maintenance of coherent historical structure.

---

## Summary

The compiled self emerges only when three conditions are simultaneously satisfied:

- Historical coherence is enforced globally  
- The substrate preserves reconstructible distinctions  
- Policies maintain viable future trajectories  

Together with local admissibility constraints, these conditions define identity as a process of continuous compilation over irreversible history rather than a fixed state.
